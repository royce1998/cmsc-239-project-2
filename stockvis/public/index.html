Learning to Compare Stocks!

Hi there! The purpose of this visualization is to help you learn which
properties of the stocks are most important to look at when deciding
to invest. In this demonstration, we will be comparing the stocks of
three tech giants, using stock data from 2016.

Visualization 1: Stock Volume

In this first visualization, we look at stock volumes. This tells
us how many share of a specific stock has been traded within a 24
hour period. In a way, the volume measure tells us how committed a
stock is towards the direction it's currently moving. When volume is
high, something big is happening to the stock (either great or terrible)
because many transctions are happening.

Try out the visualization! Click on a month to see a radial chart for the
month that compares the volume of Facebook (light blue), Google (dark
blue), and Amazon (turqoise). Note the drastic changes in dominance
from month to month. When there is not much going on at a company,
or in other words, when times are boring, there tends to be less activity
and thus less trade volume. When the company does something exciting or
something bad, there is a sudden change in people's confidence in that
company and thus there is higher trade volume.

Visualization 2: Market Capitalization

However, trade volume alone is not enough. What if there is a really
cheap stock (say $0.20 per share) but has millions of shares? Sure, it
is high in volume, but it does not necessarily have more impact than
companies like Amazon, which has lower volume but much higher price
per share. To address this issue, we use a measure called market
capitalization, which is just the market volume weighted by the price
per share across time.

Try out this visualization! Click the different months to compare market
capitalization for those months for Facebook (light blue), Google (dark
blue), and Amazon (turqoise). The interpretation is similar to the first
visualization, but now you see that the radial chart is more balanced.
Why is that? Why does Facebook tend to appear less dominant in this
visualization, as compared to the last one? This is because Facebook
stocks are much cheaper than Amazon stock prices, but are higher in
volume. By looking at the market capitalization, we get a more balanced
and reasonable (intuitively more sensible) comparison.

Visualization 3: Stock Price

One of the most common factors that everyone knows to look at is the stock
price. The lower the stock price is, the more shares you can buy with
your money. Companies that are performing well generally see a increasing
trend in their stock prices. However, there is much more that the stock
price tells us.

Use the visualization to explore the trends for stock price for Facebook,
Google, and Amazon. Use the buttons at the bottom of the page to
explore! There are two details on the graphs you should be looking at.
The first is how much variance there is in the stock price. This is
also sometimes called the volatility. The more steeper you see the lines
going up and down here, the more potentially risky it is to invest in
this stock. The second thing you should look at is the price relative
to the average price (indicated by the horizontal dotted line). If
the price is really high compared to its average, then potentially,
it can be overvalued and it may be risky to invest in. On the other
hand, if the price is low compared to its historical average, it may
be a great opportunity to invest!
